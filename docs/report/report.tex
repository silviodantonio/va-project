\section{Introduction}
% <------------- README -------------> Press Alt+Z so that the text automatically adapts to your window size, don't use the Return button to break text lines in a static manner.
According to the Italian national news, road accidents are unfortunately becoming more and more frequent and they end up involving especially young drivers.

With this in mind, we aimed to develop an intuitive way to explore data on past accidents, with the goal of identifying patterns, which could support experts in making informed decisions in order to prevent new accidents. To this end, we developed a visual analytics system designed to address this purpose. The primary intended users of this solution are members of the Italian Ministry of Infrastructure and Transport. The project could be easily extended to support other countries as well, provided they have access to similarly structured national data.

The system provides both a high level overview and a detailed view of the several types and characteristics of accidents. This is accomplished by using a high level of interactivity, which enables users to do comparisons, extract details and easily search for patterns in the dataset.

The data used was provided by ISTAT and it's relative to the road accidents with injuries and deaths that occurred in Italy during 2024 \cite{dataset}.

\section{Dataset description and preliminary work}

\subsection{How the dataset is composed}

The dataset in use contains details about vehicle accidents with injuries
occurred in Italy during 2024 \cite{dataset}. 
The attributes in the dataset are in summary: region and time (hour, weekday,
month) of accident, road section where the accident occurred (traffic circle,
intersection, etc...), type of accident (between vehicles,
vehicle-pedestrian, etc...), deadliness of the accident, and number of
accidents registered with the given characteristics.

\subsection{How data was cleaned and manipulated}

To effectively explore and interpret this dataset, we first performed a data cleaning and optimization process. A key challenge was the dataset's attributes; to address this, we implemented a dimensionality reduction strategy.
However, the fact that most of these attributes are categorical posed a
challenge when we attempted to do so.
In particular, instead of working with verbose categorical strings, we pruned the tuples to retain only integer-based indices representing specific attributes.
By operating directly on these encoded indices, we significantly minimized the computational overhead. This choice ensured higher performance and responsiveness during the interactive exploration of the dataset.
In particular, the dataset cleaned and filtered from the data
published on the ISTAT website is comprises of 92.000 tuples of 8
attributes each.

\section{Related work}

Since our very first research on the dataset, we observed that in the scientific literature there weren't many examples of visualization on the topic of road accidents.

The type of visualization that has proved to be very effective in other solutions, given the highly categorical nature of data \cite{firstPaper}, was the bar graph, which allowed to preserve an overview over the entire dataset while offering an easy way of performing data filtering. This type of visualization represented a fundamental aid to the interpretation of a map which was instead able to give the spacial distribution of the data at a glance, or to open up to completely dynamic insights. However, as one of the papers points out, numerous studies have delt with the analysis of accidents either entirely omitting visualizations or neglecting the integration of maps into their analysis. \cite{firstPaper, thirdPaper}.

Most of the studies mainly held their focus on understanding which factors could be most likely to lead to a road accident \cite{secondPaper}. So we tried as best as we could to retrieve a dataset which could express those attributes in order to build a solution which could be as near as possible to a real world tool.

Despite a data analytics solution actually built a predictive model which could return possible suggestions in order to minimize risk of new accidents \cite{fourthPaper}, none of the solutions we were able to find in the literature actually exploited an interactive visual analytics solution, not allowing the user to choose what to see and to test possible correlations among different attributes of the dataset.

This is why we tried to gather valuable information from these papers, but we tried to build our own way of presenting the data to our intended users in an interactive way, which we believe could be really helpful in the research of new accident preventing insights.

\subsection{Categorical Analysis through Interactive Bar Charts}
Bar Charts serve as the primary tool for representing categorical distributions, specifically for \textbf{Mortality Analysis}, \textbf{Regional accidents}, \textbf{Road Intersections}, and \textbf{Accident Types}. This choice leverages the human visual system's proficiency in \textit{magnitude comparison} along a common baseline, facilitating the immediate identification of the most frequent accident scenarios.
These charts implement a \textbf{dynamic dual encoding}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Visual Layering and Feedback:} Each bar displays the absolute number of accidents. Upon interaction, the system overlays a secondary bar representing the filtered subset, providing a direct visual comparison with the original distribution. Concurrently, textual labels update to show the precise percentage of the selection relative to the specific category.
    \item \textbf{Trend Indicators:} To provide statistical context, we introduced symbolic indicators (\textcolor{greater}{$\blacktriangle$}, \textcolor{less}{$\blacktriangledown$}, $\approx$). These compare the filtered subset's distribution against the national baseline, signaling whether a specific category is over-represented, under-represented, or stable.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Geospatial Mapping and Redundant Encoding}
The distribution of accidents across Italian regions is represented through a \textbf{Choropleth Map} complemented by the \textbf{Regional Bar Chart}. This intentional \textit{redundant encoding} is designed to balance numerical accuracy with spatial intuition:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Ranking and Precision:} The Bar Chart provides a quantitative ranking, allowing for a more precise comparison between regions and the identification of numerical variances that color intensity alone might obscure.
    \item \textbf{Geospatial Context:} The Choropleth Map enables the identification of macro-regional clusters and spatial correlations, providing essential geographical context that a simple ranking fails to highlight.
\end{itemize}
Through interaction with a region in the map, the region's name and exact incident count will be displayed, while clicking a region propagates a real-time filter across the entire dashboard.

\subsection{Temporal Mapping}
Temporal patterns are explored through two \textbf{Heatmaps}, which leverage color encoding to make seasonal trends and peak hours immediately visible. To facilitate the analysis, the heatmaps support a \textbf{multi-level selection logic}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Flexible Selection:} Users can interact with the matrix by selecting individual cells, entire rows, or full columns. This allows for the isolation of both specific temporal coordinates.
    
    \item \textbf{Filtering:} Any temporal selection propagates a filter in real-time across the entire dashboard.
    
    \item \textbf{Dual encoding:} Each cell provide the exact incident counts and temporal coordinates, ensuring that the visual density of the heatmap is always backed by numerical data.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Dimensionality Reduction and Brushing: PCA}
To address the high dimensionality of the dataset, we employed \textbf{Principal Component Analysis (PCA)} to project multidimensional observations—incorporating mortality, accident types, and road characteristics—into a continuous 2D space. 
Given the categorical nature of most attributes and the significant volume of the dataset, we implemented specific strategies to maintain visual clarity:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Color Encoding:} To manage the presence of identical points we utilized \textbf{color intensity} as a quantitative measure. This approach effectively visualizes data density, revealing clusters that would otherwise be hidden by \textit{overplotting}.
    
    \item \textbf{Performance Optimization:} To ensure fluid, real-time interaction, the PCA visualization is rendered using a \textbf{Canvas-based approach} rather than SVG. This allows the system to handle thousands of data points without compromising the responsiveness of the dashboard.
\end{itemize}

Interaction with this latent space is managed through \textbf{Brushing}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cluster Isolation:} By selecting a sub-region of the PCA canvas, the user can analyze specific observations sharing similar underlying characteristics.
    
    \item \textbf{Global Recalculation:} This interaction triggers a global update; all numerical values, percentages, and trend symbols in the other components are instantly recalculated to reflect the specific attributes of the brushed observations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Global Selection Analytics}
To maintain context during multi-modal exploration, a \textbf{global status indicator} provides an immediate overview of the active filters. It displays the selection in the format: \textit{Selected: X\% (N / Total)}, where both the percentage and the fraction represent the proportion of incidents currently analyzed relative to the entire dataset.

\section{Discovered insights}

The visual analytics process revealed several non-trivial patterns that distinguish fatal accidents from general road accidents based on seasonality, geography, and statistical density.

\subsection{The June Weekend Anomaly}
A significant peak occurs during the month of June. Contrary to the common perception that associates road risk with nighttime behavior, the data shows a different reality:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temporal Concentration:} Fatal accidents reach a critical peak during June weekends, accounting for $129$ mortal accidents out of a total of $2,856$ and so around  $5\% $ of the total.
    \item \textbf{Diurnal Risk:} These events do not predominantly occur at night or in the early morning. Instead, they are concentrated between \textbf{12:00 and 21:00}, with specific spikes at \textbf{16:00} and \textbf{18:00}. 
    \item \textbf{Insight:} This suggests that contrary to what is commonly believed, fatal accidents are more closely linked to leisure-related mobility and high-traffic daytime flows than to nocturnal hazards.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{ Northern Italy: Pedestrian Vulnerability}
The Northern regions exhibit a behavior diametrically opposed to the national average regarding weekly distribution:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Workweek vs. Weekend:} In Northern Italy, accidents are more frequent during the \textbf{workweek} rather than the weekend. Incidents are strictly correlated with commuting hours, specifically \textbf{08:00--09:00} and \textbf{18:00--19:00}.
    \item \textbf{Pedestrian Trend:} These regions show a higher-than-average trend for \textbf{vehicle-pedestrian} accidents compared to the rest of the country, highlighting a specific vulnerability in urban mobility management.
    \item \textbf{Insight:} The data suggests that in Northern Italy, road risk is an commuting hours byproduct rather than a leisure-related phenomenon. The fact that pedestrian risk is higher in the "rush hour" pressure, indicates that interventions should prioritize the de-confliction of pedestrian and vehicular flows during the peaks hours.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{ Seasonal Inversion: Cold vs. Warm Months}
Cross-referencing the time of year with the type of accident revealed a systematic trend reversal:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cold Months:} These months record a number of \textbf{vehicle-pedestrian} accidents significantly above the national average, while vehicle-to-vehicle collisions remain below average.
    \item \textbf{Warm Months:} The pattern inverts completely. Summer months see a dominance of vehicle-to-vehicle collisions and a sharp decrease in pedestrian-involved fatalities.
    \item \textbf{Insight:} This inversion reflects that winter risk is concentrated in high-density urban commuting where visibility is lower, whereas summer shifts the risk toward long-distance travel and inter-city mobility, effectively transferring the danger from the sidewalk to the highway. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{ PCA Analysis: The Statistical Uniqueness of Fatalities}
The application of Principal Component Analysis (PCA) provided a fundamental insight into the nature of fatal accidents:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Low Density:} Fatal accidents exhibit extremely low commonality. In the PCA projection, these events consistently show a \textbf{density of 1} and a \textbf{number of observations equal to 1}.
    \item \textbf{Analytical Conclusion:} This indicates that fatal accidents are effectively statistical ``outliers" or unique events. Unlike non-fatal accidents, which often cluster around repetitive patterns, each fatal incident is characterized by a unique combination of variables, suggesting that prevention requires a highly multifactorial approach.
\end{itemize}
\section{Conclusions and future work}
This project provides an analysis of road accidents, highlighting how temporal and geographical factors significantly influence accident typologies. To further enhance the current solution, even more functionalities can be adde, such as:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multi-category filtering:} Future integration will allow for simultaneous multiple selections within the same attribute. This will enable more complex cross-referencing of data belonging to the same category or "type" for deeper comparative analysis.
    \item \textbf{Per capita normalization:} The current map visualization, which displays the absolute accident counts, could be integrated with a \textbf{per capita} view.
    By weighing accidents against the regional population, it is possible to provide another and different representation of relative risk across italian's regions.
    \item \textbf{Temporal expansion:} While the current analysis focuses on 2024 data, future updates might include \textbf{multi-year selection}. This expansion could allow users to track trends over a longer period of time and evaluate the impact of law changes across different years.
\end{itemize}
